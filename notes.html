smFC preprint is out on bioRxiv
Amir Rahmani · University of Cambridge · August 2025 · News

Our preprint on single-molecule flow cytometry (smFC) is now live on bioRxiv. We integrated high-NA oblique plane microscopy with microfluidics to push flow cytometry sensitivity down to single molecules on live cells — detecting as few as ~2 labelled receptors per cell.

Our preprint on single-molecule flow cytometry (smFC) is now live on bioRxiv. We integrated high-NA oblique plane microscopy with microfluidics to push flow cytometry sensitivity down to single molecules on live cells — detecting as few as ~2 labelled receptors per cell.

The key insight was combining optical sectioning from the OPM light sheet with super-bright, large Stokes-shift fluorophores to overcome cellular autofluorescence — which is the main bottleneck in conventional flow cytometry at low expression levels. We validated the system on c-kit receptor distribution in triple-negative breast cancer cells and found a subpopulation invisible to standard FC.

We're now working on extending smFC to multicolour detection and higher throughput. Feedback very welcome — reach out or leave a comment on bioRxiv.

Why solvatochromic dyes are tricky in cold membranes
Amir Rahmani · University of Cambridge · January 2026 · Lab Note

Working with solvatochromic dyes like di-4-ANEPPDHQ at sub-zero temperatures throws up some surprising challenges. Membrane fluidity drops dramatically as you cool cells toward 0°C and below, which is precisely what makes Antarctic fish cells so interesting — but it also changes how the dye partitions and reports.

The emission spectrum of solvatochromic dyes shifts with the polarity of their local environment. In fluid (disordered) membranes, the dye sits in a more polar environment and shifts one way; in ordered (gel-phase) membranes, it shifts the other. The problem at very low temperatures is that most mammalian membrane lipids start to phase-separate or gel, and the spectral shift you'd normally interpret as "ordered membrane" now partially reflects a kinetic freezing-in of the dye rather than true equilibrium partitioning.

One practical implication: you need to allow longer equilibration times at low temperature before imaging, and you should always include a positive control (a cell line with known membrane order) at the same temperature. More notes to follow as this project develops.

Reading: soTILT3D — whole-cell SMLM without a second objective
Amir Rahmani · University of Cambridge · December 2024 · Reading

A recent Nature Communications paper presents soTILT3D — a single-objective tilted light-sheet approach for whole-cell 3D SMLM. It is a clever engineering solution to a problem I've spent a lot of time thinking about: how do you get optical sectioning and high-NA collection in the same objective without a second lens getting in the way?

The key idea is to steer a light sheet at a tilt angle through the sample using only the primary high-NA objective, combined with nanoprinted microfluidics to hold the sample. This avoids the stitching artefacts and drift issues that plague dual-objective setups. The results show super-resolution imaging of whole mammalian cells with reduced background compared to TIRF or HILO.

What I find most interesting is the nanoprinted microfluidics integration — it essentially solves the sample-mounting headache that has always been the limiting factor for single-objective light-sheet designs. Worth keeping in mind for the cryo-imaging project, where sample mounting at sub-zero temperatures is its own engineering challenge.

On building microscopes vs. buying them
Amir Rahmani · University of Leeds · October 2023 · Thought

A recurring debate in imaging labs: should you build your own microscope or buy a commercial system? The honest answer depends on what question you are trying to answer — but I've come to think the framing itself is slightly wrong.

A recurring debate in imaging labs: should you build your own microscope or buy a commercial system? The honest answer depends on what question you are trying to answer — but I've come to think the framing itself is slightly wrong.

Commercial systems are optimised for robustness, reproducibility, and throughput. They are excellent for asking well-defined questions on well-behaved samples. The moment your sample or your question is at the edge of what the instrument was designed for — flowing cells, sub-zero temperatures, nanometre-precision focus over long timescales — you are fighting the machine rather than using it.

Building your own system is slow and often frustrating, but it forces you to understand every degree of freedom in the measurement. When something goes wrong (and it always does), you know where to look. More importantly, when you want to do something the machine was not designed for, you can. The PiFocus project started because I needed focus stabilisation compatible with a 10× objective — something no commercial autofocus system supported at the time.

My working rule: buy commercial for routine measurements, build custom for novel modalities. And never underestimate how much you learn from building.
