I'm a Postdoctoral Research Associate in the Department of Chemical Engineering and Biotechnology at the University of Cambridge, working in Prof. Clemens Kaminski's group in collaboration with the British Antarctic Survey on a UKRI-funded project. My research sits at the intersection of physics, biology, and engineering. I develop advanced super-resolution microscopy tools to study nanoscale cellular behaviour at sub-zero temperatures.

I hold a PhD in Physics from the University of Leeds, where I developed the first single-molecule flow cytometry system for applications in adaptive immunotherapy. Prior to my doctoral studies, I trained at world-leading institutes including EMBL Heidelberg and ICFO Barcelona, shaping my passion for cutting-edge imaging science. Beyond research, I enjoy mentoring students, teaching, and engaging with the broader scientific community.

Research

My work spans advanced fluorescence microscopy, biophysics, and biological imaging, with a focus on developing custom optical systems to address biological questions at the nanoscale. My current project brings together the Kaminski Group and the British Antarctic Survey to develop a high-resolution microscopy platform capable of visualising protein dynamics in Antarctic fish cells at sub-zero temperatures. Solvatochromic probes allow us to sense changes in membrane organisation in real time, without perturbing the cell. I also develop a computational pipeline on top of Nellie's output to extract quantitative morphological parameters of mitochondrial networks for high-throughput batch analysis.
